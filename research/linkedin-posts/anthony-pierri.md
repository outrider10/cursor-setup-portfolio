## Post 1

**Date:** ~6h prior to capture

**Topic:** Why positioning "best practices" are context-dependent, not universal rules

### Summary

Pierri challenges commonly repeated startup and positioning "rules" by pairing each one with well-known companies that succeeded by breaking it. He cites the advice to "never compete on price" against counterexamples like discount and budget-focused brands, "never release a product that's actually a feature" against companies whose offerings could be framed that way, and "always start with a huge total addressable market" against companies that began in narrow niches before scaling. His broader point is that positioning and go-to-market advice is inherently contextual: a tactic that worked for one company can be actively wrong for another, depending on their specific market, timing, and constraints. He frames this variability not as a flaw but as an inherent feature of startup strategy, contrasting the ambiguity of startup decision-making with the relative certainty of more rule-bound professions.

### Key Takeaways

- Positioning and strategy "best practices" are frequently contradicted by successful real-world examples, showing they are heuristics rather than universal laws.
- The same strategic choice (e.g., competing on price, building what looks like a feature, targeting a narrow market) can be either a strength or a liability depending on a company's specific context.
- Blindly applying generic startup advice without evaluating fit to your own market and constraints is a common positioning mistake.
- Comparative examples (naming specific companies on both sides of a "rule") are an effective way to demonstrate nuance and challenge oversimplified frameworks.
- Embracing ambiguity and context-dependent decision-making is presented as a core, unavoidable part of building a differentiated company.

## Post 2

**Date:** ~1 day prior to capture

**Topic:** Replacing the traditional sales deck with a fully transparent, self-service consultancy homepage

### Summary

Pierri describes how his consultancy conducts sales calls directly from its homepage instead of using a traditional sales deck, deliberately front-loading information — including pricing — that most consulting firms typically withhold. He notes that prospects are often surprised, and slightly embarrassed, to learn pricing is openly listed, since they're used to consultancy websites relying on vague language and generic imagery rather than concrete detail. He outlines five specific elements his homepage is structured around: core positioning (who the offer is for and what it does), pricing and packaging, a self-diagnostic tool to help visitors determine if they actually have the problem being solved, tangible deliverables, and a clear description of the engagement process. He credits a podcast discussion on creative-firm website design as partial inspiration and notes he's planning to add further transparency elements like staff photos and a speaking page.

### Key Takeaways

- Publishing pricing openly on a homepage can differentiate a consultancy in a category where opacity is the norm, and can pre-qualify or accelerate the sales conversation.
- A homepage can be structured to function as a self-contained sales tool, replacing the need for a separate sales deck if it clearly covers positioning, pricing, self-qualification, deliverables, and process.
- A self-diagnostic checklist ("do you actually have this problem?") helps prospects self-qualify before a sales call, improving the quality of inbound conversations.
- Making deliverables and process explicit and tangible builds more trust than vague, high-level descriptions common in professional-services marketing.
- Transparency itself can function as a positioning differentiator in industries where competitors default to ambiguity.

## Overall Research Notes

Across both posts, Anthony Pierri consistently emphasizes that effective positioning depends on rejecting generic rules and defaults in favor of clarity, specificity, and honesty tailored to a company's actual context and audience. His first post shows this at the level of strategic advice — arguing that positioning "best practices" only work situationally — while his second post applies the same philosophy operationally, using radical homepage transparency (pricing, self-qualification, deliverables, process) to differentiate from an industry norm of vagueness. His overall approach treats positioning not as a static statement but as something that must be proven through concrete, tangible communication — whether that's naming real counterexamples to conventional wisdom or replacing a sales deck with a fully transparent website.
