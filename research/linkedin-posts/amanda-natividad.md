## Post 1

**Date:** ~2h prior to capture

**Topic:** "Ghost citations" — when AI uses your content without naming your brand

### Summary

Natividad explains a concept she attributes to Kevin Indig: "ghost citations," where an AI system retrieves and relies on a brand's content to generate an answer but never actually names that brand in the response. She illustrates this with an example from Indig's podcast appearance: a query like "what's the best CRM" may draw heavily on a review platform's data to construct the answer, yet the response is far more likely to name specific CRM brands than to credit the platform that supplied the underlying information. She notes this dynamic may be acceptable for review/data platforms whose business model depends on being a credible backend source rather than a consumer-facing brand, but argues it has broader implications for how marketers should measure AI visibility. Rather than relying on citation counts alone, she suggests brands should also track whether they're named directly, whether they appear in shortlists, and whether they're associated with the specific attributes that matter in their category. She frames ghost citations as the AI-search equivalent of zero-click search behavior: content can shape outcomes and demand without the source brand ever becoming visible.

### Key Takeaways

- A "ghost citation" occurs when an AI system uses a brand's content to form an answer without naming that brand, even if the content was clearly the basis for the response.
- Citation-based AI visibility metrics can be misleading on their own, since being used as a source doesn't guarantee being named or credited.
- For some business models (e.g., data/review platforms), ghost citations may be an acceptable trade-off; for others, invisibility in the named response could be a real demand-generation risk.
- A more complete AI-visibility framework should track direct name mentions, shortlist appearances, and association with category-relevant attributes — not citations alone.
- Ghost citations parallel "zero-click" search behavior: a brand's content can influence outcomes and shape user decisions while the brand itself remains unseen and unattributed.

## Post 2

**Date:** ~6 days prior to capture

**Topic:** AI visibility as a new leading indicator of demand *(sponsored content — HubSpot AEO partnership)*

### Summary

Natividad argues that as buyers increasingly turn to AI tools for recommendations and comparisons, a brand's presence in AI-generated answers functions as an early signal of whether it's even being considered before a prospect visits its website. She references a Similarweb study on the "downstream impact of AI visibility," which found that brands recommended in AI answers saw increased site visits in the following week — though this impact typically appeared as branded search or direct traffic rather than a clean, attributable AI referral click. She frames this as real but "messy" demand that's difficult to attribute using traditional analytics. She outlines a set of questions marketers should ask about their AI visibility: whether they appear at all, whether they're described accurately, whether they're compared to the right competitors, whether their strongest proof points are represented, and whether they're present in the moments where buyer preferences are actually formed. The post closes with a sponsored mention of HubSpot's AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) tool, positioned as a way to track and improve visibility across AI platforms like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity.

### Key Takeaways

- AI visibility functions as an early indicator of buyer consideration, occurring before traditional website analytics can detect any engagement.
- The impact of AI-driven recommendations often shows up indirectly, as increased branded search or direct traffic, rather than as directly attributable referral clicks — making it harder to measure with standard tools.
- A comprehensive AI-visibility check should assess not just presence, but accuracy of description, competitive framing, and inclusion of key proof points.
- Being entirely absent from AI-generated answers risks missing the specific moment where buyer shortlists and preferences are formed, not just missing a click.
- This post is sponsored content promoting a specific vendor tool (HubSpot AEO) as one method for tracking and improving AI-search visibility, and should be read with that commercial framing in mind.

## Overall Research Notes

Across both posts, Amanda Natividad consistently emphasizes that traditional, click-based measurement frameworks are insufficient for understanding how AI-mediated search is shaping buyer behavior and brand demand. Her core argument is that visibility and influence in AI answers can exist independently of citations, clicks, or directly attributable traffic — meaning marketers need new, more nuanced ways to track whether they're named, accurately described, and positioned favorably at the exact moment AI tools are forming a buyer's consideration set. This is directly relevant to AI-Powered SEO Content Production research: her "ghost citation" framing and demand-indicator argument both highlight a core measurement challenge unique to the AI-search era — that content can perform well upstream (informing AI answers) while still failing to build brand-level recognition or attributable traffic downstream. Her work is a useful reference for teams building AI-search content strategies, since it reframes the goal of AI-optimized content beyond simply "getting cited" toward the more strategic questions of brand naming, accurate framing, and competitive positioning within AI-generated answers.
