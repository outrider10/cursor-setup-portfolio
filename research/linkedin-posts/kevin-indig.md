## Post 1

**Date:** 2026-07-08

**Topic:** Inconsistent brand citations across different AI search engines ("the ghost citation problem")
 
### Summary

In this carousel post, Indig describes what he calls the "ghost citation problem": a brand can be cited or ranked prominently by one AI answer engine while being entirely absent from another, even for comparable queries. He frames this as evidence that AI search engines do not share a unified index or citation logic the way traditional search engines converge on similar rankings. The inconsistency creates a new kind of visibility risk for brands, since strong performance in one AI system offers no guarantee of visibility elsewhere. Indig positions this as a core challenge for teams optimizing content for AI-driven discovery, since it undermines a single-engine mindset to SEO and demands cross-engine testing and monitoring.
 
### Key Takeaways

- Different AI search/answer engines can produce wildly divergent citation and ranking outcomes for the same brand or topic.
- Being cited favorably by one AI engine does not translate into consistent visibility across other AI platforms.
- Content and SEO strategy can no longer assume convergence across search systems the way it historically could with traditional Google-centric SEO.
- Brands need to monitor presence across multiple AI engines individually rather than relying on a single benchmark or citation source.
- This fragmentation adds a new layer of unpredictability to AI-driven discovery and demand generation strategies.

## Post 2

**Date:** ~July 2026 (11h prior to capture)

**Topic:** SERP scraper pollution in Google Search Console data
 
### Summary

Kevin Indig flags a growing data-quality issue in Google Search Console: automated SERP-scraping bots are inflating impression counts without generating any real clicks, which distorts the reliability of organic search reporting. He illustrates this with a Search Console screenshot showing a sharp, isolated spike of roughly 28,000 impressions with zero clicks over a short recent window. The spike is tied to a cluster of near-identical, machine-generated queries repeatedly evaluating a specific edtech brand across templated phrasings (e.g., variations on "homework help," "study apps," and "citation generator"). Indig argues this pattern reflects rank-tracking or LLM-training scraper traffic rather than genuine user search behavior, and warns that this kind of noise can meaningfully distort impression-based SEO metrics if left unaccounted for.
 
### Key Takeaways

- Search Console impression data can be significantly polluted by automated scraper and rank-tracking bots that never result in real clicks.
- A telltale sign of scraper activity is a sudden impression spike with a flat or zero click rate, isolated to a short time window.
- Synthetic, templated query patterns (same brand name, repeated modifiers) are a strong indicator of bot-driven rather than organic search volume.
- Marketers should treat spikes in impressions with unusually low CTR as a data-quality flag before drawing conclusions about content performance or ranking changes.
- This trend has implications for how AI training/rank-tracking crawlers are reshaping the reliability of traditional SEO analytics tools.

## Overall Research Notes

Across both posts, Kevin Indig consistently emphasizes that the mechanics underlying search visibility are becoming less stable and less standardized as AI systems reshape how content is crawled, indexed, and surfaced. His first post highlights a measurement-layer problem — automated scraper traffic corrupting the reliability of Search Console data — while his second highlights a strategy-layer problem — the unpredictable, non-uniform way different AI engines cite and rank brands. The throughline is a philosophy of skepticism toward legacy SEO assumptions: metrics that once reliably signaled organic performance, and ranking behavior that once converged predictably across search engines, can no longer be trusted at face value in an AI-mediated search landscape. This makes Indig highly relevant to AI-Powered SEO Content Production research, since he is actively documenting the operational and analytical challenges brands face when adapting content strategy, measurement practices, and demand generation efforts to a multi-engine, AI-driven discovery environment. His work is a useful signal for practitioners building AI content workflows who need to account for both data integrity risks and engine-specific visibility variance rather than assuming a single-engine optimization model still applies.
