## Post 1

**Date:** ~5h prior to capture

**Topic:** Distinguishing positioning, messaging, and narratives — and mapping narratives by content depth instead of funnel stage
 
### Summary

Faus draws a clear distinction between three commonly conflated marketing concepts: positioning (a brand or product's defined place and unique value in the market), messaging or a "message house" (the translation of that positioning into consistent, cohesive communication tied directly to product benefits), and narratives (broader, product-agnostic stories about a problem and solution space that provide context and should hold value even for those who never buy). Her core argument is that the traditional marketing funnel is a retrospective measurement tool, not a forward-looking planning framework, since real audiences move through a non-linear "playground" rather than a straight path to purchase. Because of this, she proposes organizing narrative content by depth rather than funnel stage: conceptual content addressing the "what" and "why," strategic content covering process and key knowledge components, and tactical content offering prescriptive, step-by-step execution. The accompanying materials illustrate this with a fitness-industry example, showing how the same underlying idea (e.g., protein and strength training) can be expressed conceptually (general diet and muscle goals), strategically (lifting form, protein sourcing), and tactically (specific bicep exercises, specific recipes).
 
### Key Takeaways

- Positioning, messaging, and narrative are distinct layers: positioning defines market value, messaging operationalizes it consistently across channels, and narrative provides broader, product-agnostic context and meaning.
- A strong narrative should add value to an audience even if they never become customers, since audiences who don't align with the underlying point of view will never convert or stay retained.
- The funnel should be treated as a retrospective measurement tool rather than a forward-looking content strategy, since real audience behavior is non-linear.
- Organizing content by "depth" (conceptual, strategic, tactical) rather than funnel stage allows a brand to maintain a cohesive narrative regardless of which path an individual audience member takes.
- The same core narrative idea can and should be expressed at multiple depths simultaneously (e.g., a philosophy-level idea, a how-it-works explanation, and a step-by-step guide) to serve different audience needs without fragmenting the underlying story.

## Post 2

**Date:** ~2 days prior to capture

**Topic:** Using job market data as a competitive-intelligence tool for tracking industry and role positioning trends
 
### Summary

Faus describes a practice of regularly reviewing job descriptions in her field as a way to monitor how marketing roles, skill expectations, and industry focus areas are shifting over time. She outlines a specific set of questions she tracks: volume of relevant openings, the profile of hiring companies (size, stage, industry, location), seniority levels, listed responsibilities and success metrics compared to her own current scope, required qualifications compared to her own background, how role descriptions have changed over the past year, and how compensation ranges have shifted. She credits this practice with informing a past graduate-education decision and with helping her identify a longer-term industry pattern: a roughly ten-year pendulum swing between specialist and generalist skill sets, which she observes is currently consolidating under the "Product Marketing" title. The post reframes job-description review from a purely job-seeking activity into an ongoing market-intelligence habit for understanding where one's professional positioning stands relative to the broader field.
 
### Key Takeaways

- Job postings can function as a structured, low-cost data source for tracking how skill demand, seniority expectations, and compensation are shifting within a profession or industry.
- Comparing one's current responsibilities and qualifications against live job listings offers a practical, ongoing way to benchmark personal or professional positioning against market expectations.
- Tracking descriptions over time (not just a single snapshot) reveals longer-term structural patterns, such as cyclical shifts between generalist and specialist skill demand.
- This kind of market-scanning habit can directly inform high-stakes career decisions (e.g., choice of further education) by grounding them in observed industry trends rather than assumptions.
- The practice illustrates a broader principle relevant to marketers: understanding shifts in your own field's positioning and narrative trends is as valuable as understanding shifts in a target customer's market.

## Overall Research Notes

Across both posts, Ashley Faus consistently emphasizes structured, intentional thinking about how ideas are organized and communicated — whether that's distinguishing positioning from messaging from narrative in brand communication, or systematically tracking how professional roles and skill expectations evolve over time. Her overall philosophy treats audiences (whether customers or industry peers) as moving non-linearly through information, which means marketers must build cohesive, multi-depth narrative systems rather than rigid, funnel-stage-bound content plans. This makes her highly relevant to AI-Powered SEO Content Production research: her positioning/messaging/narrative framework offers a direct, reusable model for structuring AI-assisted content programs so that conceptual, strategic, and tactical content pieces reinforce a single cohesive narrative rather than becoming disconnected, funnel-stage-siloed assets — a common risk when content is produced at scale with AI. Her second post additionally models a transferable research habit (systematic job-description analysis) that content and marketing teams could adapt to monitor shifts in AI search behavior, content formats, or buyer expectations over time, reinforcing her broader emphasis on grounding strategy in observed patterns rather than assumption.
